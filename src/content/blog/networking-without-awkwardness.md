Most people dread networking because they treat it as improvisation. They show up, hope for chemistry, and feel embarrassed when the conversation stalls. The fix is to rehearse before you arrive—not by memorizing a script, but by internalizing a structure.

## The four pillars of a strong intro

Every networking conversation has four components, whether you plan them or not:

1. **Rapport spark** — a genuine observation or shared context that starts the conversation.
2. **Identity line** — a one-sentence description of who you are and what you are working toward.
3. **Value proof** — a brief example that shows you can do what you claim.
4. **Curious ask** — a question that gives the other person a chance to share expertise.

## Why rehearsal works

Rehearsal does not make you robotic. It makes you available. When you are not searching for words, you can listen. When you know your own story, you can adapt it to the person in front of you.

## The two-minute drill

Set a timer for two minutes. Deliver your intro aloud. Then rate yourself on:

- Did the identity line sound like you, or like a LinkedIn headline?
- Was the value proof specific enough to be memorable?
- Did the curious ask invite a real answer, or just a polite one?
- Did you sound like you were reciting, or like you were sharing?

Repeat the drill with different scenarios: a career fair, a coffee chat, a virtual event. Each context rewards a slightly different tone.
