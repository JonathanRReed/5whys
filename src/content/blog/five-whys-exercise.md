The five whys exercise is a deceptively simple tool. You ask "why" five times in succession, and each answer should dig deeper than the last. By the fifth layer, you often find the real motivation behind a career move—not the surface-level reason you tell yourself.

## Why it works

Most career decisions are made with incomplete information. You think you want a new role because of the salary, but the real driver might be autonomy, recognition, or escape from a toxic environment. The five whys exposes these hidden drivers.

## How to do it

Start with a statement about your next move. Then ask why five times, writing each answer before moving to the next layer.

**Example:**

1. *I want to move to a product management role.*  
   → Why? Because I want more influence over what gets built.

2. *Why do you want more influence?*  
   → Because I am tired of building features no one uses.

3. *Why does that bother you?*  
   → Because my best work happens when I can see the impact.

4. *Why is impact important to you?*  
   → Because I need to feel like my time produces something real.

5. *Why does that matter so much?*  
   → Because I grew up watching my parents work hard with nothing to show for it. I promised myself I would not repeat that.

By layer five, the real driver is clear: the need for tangible, visible impact rooted in a personal promise.

## Common mistakes

- **Stopping at layer two or three.** The first layers are usually socially acceptable answers. Keep going.
- **Answering with another job title.** "Because I want to be a director" is not a why. It is a what.
- **Rushing.** This works best when you write slowly and let each answer sit for a moment.

## What to do with the result

Your final answer is not a job description. It is a criterion. Use it to evaluate roles, not just to daydream about them. When a new opportunity looks attractive, run it through your five-whys filter: does it serve the real motivation you uncovered?
